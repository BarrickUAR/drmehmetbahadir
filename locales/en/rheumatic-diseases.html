Rheumatic Diseases

Psoriasis

Psoriasis is a disease characterized by redness and rash-like symptoms on the skin. Affecting approximately 1 million people in Turkey, it is a non-contagious disease often mistaken as contagious due to its visible skin symptoms. Individuals with the disease are frequently excluded from social life due to the physical symptoms.

Psoriasis has different types determined by specific characteristics. It is classified into plaque, inverse, erythrodermic, and guttate types based on the areas and severity of the symptoms. The plaque type, which causes redness on the knees and elbows, is the most common form.

Eczema

Eczema is a type of skin disease, but it is not dangerous. One in 12 people has experienced eczema, making it a common condition.

It is natural to experience itching with eczema, but it is not overly bothersome. However, it can cause visual discomfort when it appears on certain areas of the body.

Lichen Planus

Lichen Planus is an inflammatory disease of the skin and mucous membranes with an unknown cause but a strong immunopathogenetic component. It develops as itchy, ring-shaped, flat-topped papules in a lilac color, sometimes with a white, lace-like surface. The papules can be few or numerous.

30% of patients have a white network on the oral mucosa, and lesions can also appear in the genital area.

There are micropapules and hypertrophic forms. Sometimes, scalp involvement can lead to alopecia. Nails can also be affected.

Histologically, there is damage to the basal layer of the epidermis. Civatte bodies and a band-like infiltration of lymphocytes and histiocytes are observed in the subepidermis. It is thought to be an autoimmune disease.

Topical corticosteroids can help relieve itching, but the disease may resolve on its own.

Lichen Planus is a skin disease believed to have psychological origins, though no definitive cause has been identified. It commonly affects the inner skin (mouth, throat, esophagus) and genital areas in both men and women. It is also associated with liver disorders. Women are more affected than men, and the most common age for complaints is between 30 and 50 years.

The disease starts with itching, followed by raised lesions that turn purple. In the mouth, it appears as small, white lesions. Patients mainly complain of severe itching. In advanced cases, Lichen Planus can lead to cancer in the mouth.

Ichthyosis

Keratinization is the transformation of underlying epidermal cells into hard, shield-like corneocytes that form the stratum corneum, which acts as a barrier.

Scaling results from the failure of corneocytes to separate from the skin during the final stage of keratinization. Thus, scaling occurs due to the accumulation of corneocytes that cannot be shed. Keratinization disorders can be congenital or acquired. Scaling can cause significant physical and psychological discomfort. Xeroderma, commonly known as dry skin, is a true form of skin scaling and can result from keratinization disorders. It is common in the elderly and those with atopic eczema, worsening in low-humidity winter conditions.

Treatment for xeroderma involves frequent use of moisturizers and moisturizing cleansers, as well as taking showers instead of baths.

Autosomal dominant ichthyosis is characterized by fine, widespread scaling, worse on the extensor surfaces of the body. Keratosis pilaris may accompany this condition, with keratin plugs in hair follicle canals. Its prevalence is 0.2%, and its biochemical cause is not fully understood. Moisturizers and keratolytic agents can sometimes be helpful.

Scaling in X-linked ichthyosis is more pronounced. Affected male children are often postmature and born after difficult deliveries. The metabolic disorder is primarily due to steroid sulfatase deficiency, detectable in fibroblast, epidermal, and lymphocyte cultures. Women are carriers as it is X-linked recessive, with a prevalence of 1 in 6,000.

Non-bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma is rare, inherited autosomal recessively, and characterized by widespread erythema and fine scaling. Oral acitretin can help some patients despite severe side effects.

Rare bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma (also known as epidermolytic hyperkeratosis) shows widespread erythema, folded hyperkeratosis, and a tendency to blister. There are characteristic degenerative changes in the upper epidermis. The disorder is autosomal dominant, with known keratin gene mutations. Oral retinoids improve appearance but may increase blistering.

Lamellar ichthyosis is a rare autosomal recessive disorder characterized by severe hyperkeratosis and scaling.

Patients with non-bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma, epidermolytic hyperkeratosis, and lamellar ichthyosis may be born with a collodion membrane. This shiny, transparent membrane peels off after a week. Rarely, it can also occur in normal infants. Careful care is needed due to impaired skin barrier function.

Acquired ichthyosis can occur with lymphoma, HIV, leprosy, essential fatty acid deficiency, and the use of certain lipid-lowering drugs.

Darier's disease is rare and can be autosomal dominant or sporadic. Brownish, scaly papules appear on the upper body and face. Palmar pits and nail dystrophy also occur. Histologically, suprabasal separation and premature keratinization are evident.

Tuberous sclerosis is rare and an autosomal dominant disorder with multiple anomalies. Pink-red papules around the nose and cheeks are characteristic.

Von Recklinghausen's disease (neurofibromatosis) is a not uncommon autosomal dominant disorder. Soft, pinkish tumors develop, and brown "cafe au lait" spots may accompany the condition.

Ichthyosis Vulgaris, commonly known as fish scale disease, is a skin condition where dead skin cells accumulate, creating a fish-scale-like appearance.

Diseased genes from parents can cause the condition in their child. Symptoms may appear shortly after birth or during childhood.

Symptoms of Ichthyosis include dry skin, fish-scale-like formations, and thickening of the skin, often on the elbows and knees but sometimes spreading to the arms and hands.
