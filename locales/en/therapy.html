Phytotherapy

Phytotherapy refers to the use of plants for the prevention and treatment of diseases. Contrary to popular belief, phytotherapy is not a treatment without side effects. On the contrary, plant-based treatments can have intense side effects. In our country, there are dozens of patients and their relatives who apply uncontrolled herbal treatments and end up in hospitals due to toxication. The side effects of phytotherapy using standardized, or rather, drug-like extraction methods are limited.

Phytotherapy is a specialized field in medicine. In Turkey, the authority to use phytotherapy (herbal treatment) for diseases belongs to medical doctors who have received phytotherapy training from the Ministry of Health. It is a crime for untrained individuals (such as herbalists or chemists) to apply herbal treatments to patients, and there are severe legal penalties for this.

Unfortunately, despite its rich flora, Turkey is far behind the world in the use and application of phytotherapy. However, some progress has been made in recent years due to reforms by the Ministry of Health.

In developed countries, the direct use of chemical drugs for diseases has become outdated. The new approach in medicine is to first use dietary supplements for disease prevention, followed by herbal drugs, and then chemical or other radical treatments if necessary. For example, upper respiratory tract infections are the most common ailment in young children, accounting for 85% of cases. Considering how often a child falls ill during a winter season, we can understand how much antibiotics their body is exposed to. However, upper respiratory tract infections can easily respond to herbal treatments without harming the child's body.

Herbal treatments are much more effective than antibiotics and other chemical drugs. For example, applying a mixture of peppermint oil on the back of a 6-7-year-old child before bedtime can significantly reduce their cough in a very short time. From this perspective, phytotherapy is one of the most effective factors in complementary medicine.

Looking at the world from our country, we see that in countries like China, India, and other Asian nations, nearly two-thirds of pharmacy shelves are filled with herbal roots. Similarly, in the United States, one of the most advanced countries in healthcare, three-quarters of pharmacies are stocked with herbal drugs.

In short, phytotherapy (herbal treatment) is a method that requires expertise in our country and will leave a significant mark on the treatment of diseases in the coming period.

Ozone Therapy

Ozone therapy is one of the traditional complementary medicine methods that has gained popularity worldwide. It is widely used and in demand in countries such as the United States, European countries, Russia, Japan, Canada, and many others. In Turkey, it has been integrated into modern medicine practices under the Ministry of Health's GETAT (Traditional and Complementary Medicine) regulations.

To understand ozone therapy, one must first understand oxygen. Oxygen is an indispensable source of life for all living beings, especially humans. Our metabolism constantly requires oxygen, and for a quality life, we need to breathe 10,000 liters of air daily. Oxygen deficiency can lead to fatigue, weakness, diseases, rapid aging, and immune system disorders.

The term "ozone" comes from the Greek word "ozein," meaning "to smell." Ozone is a type of oxygen with three oxygen atoms instead of two, making it an unstable gas. The single oxygen atom (O) released during its breakdown interacts rapidly with organic materials, and this property is utilized in ozone therapy.

Ozone (O3), an active form of oxygen, provides high energy by oxygenating blood and tissues. It fights viruses, bacteria, and toxins, destroying damaged and diseased cells while defending the body against diseases. In short, ozone can be described as oxygen filled with high energy.

Ozone gas consists of three oxygen atoms and is a colorless gas with a sharp, characteristic odor. It has strong disinfecting and oxidizing properties. Medical ozone, used in complementary medicine, is different from natural ozone and is created by combining pure oxygen and ozone in specific ratios. It is administered to patients in controlled doses, similar to medications.

Ozone therapy is a complementary treatment that promotes cell regeneration and strengthens the body. Oxygen is essential for all bodily functions, and oxygen deficiency can disrupt blood flow and organ function. Ozone therapy accelerates the healing process by providing concentrated energy to damaged areas.

Side effects of ozone therapy are rare, with only six cases reported per million treatments, making it a safe and reliable complementary treatment method.

Mesotherapy

Mesotherapy, derived from the Latin words "meso" (middle) and "therapy" (treatment), involves injecting a substance just below the skin using short, thin needles. Although the term "therapy" is used, it refers to the injection of a substance rather than the treatment of a disease. It is particularly effective in areas where traditional surface treatments fail.

As we age, our skin tends to sag and wrinkle, especially if it is dry. Mesotherapy aims to rejuvenate the face and lift the skin.

Mesotherapy can effectively address the following issues:

* Age-related wrinkles
* Under-eye bags
* Skin laxity and swelling
* Acne
* Age spots
* Spider veins
* Cellulite and stretch marks
* Hair loss and dandruff...

Benefits of mesotherapy include:
* Reduction of skin problems, resulting in a youthful appearance and healthier skin tone.
* Elimination of inflammation and acne.
* Improvement in oily skin and reduction of pore size.
* Significant reduction in deep wrinkles and complete disappearance of fine lines.

How is mesotherapy performed?
* Mesotherapy is performed by specialists using ultra-thin needles for injections.
* Alternatively, meso cocktails can be applied using special devices without injections.

Ichthyosis

Keratinization is the process by which epidermal cells transform into hard, shield-like corneocytes that form the stratum corneum, the skin's barrier layer.

Scaling occurs when corneocytes fail to shed from the skin, leading to their accumulation. This can be congenital or acquired and may cause significant physical and psychological discomfort. Xeroderma, commonly known as dry skin, is a form of scaling that worsens in low-humidity conditions, such as winter.

Autosomal dominant ichthyosis is characterized by fine, widespread scaling, often worse on extensor surfaces. Keratosis pilaris may accompany this condition, with keratin plugs in hair follicles. Its prevalence is 0.2%, and its biochemical cause is not fully understood.

X-linked ichthyosis features more pronounced scaling. Affected males are often born postmaturely. The metabolic disorder is linked to steroid sulfatase deficiency. Females are carriers, as it is X-linked recessive, with a prevalence of 1 in 6,000.

Non-bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma is rare and autosomal recessive, characterized by widespread erythema and fine scaling. Oral acitretin may help despite its side effects.

Bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma (epidermolytic hyperkeratosis) is rare and autosomal dominant, featuring widespread erythema, folded hyperkeratosis, and blistering. Oral retinoids improve appearance but may increase blistering.

Lamellar ichthyosis is a rare autosomal recessive disorder with severe hyperkeratosis and scaling.

Patients with non-bullous ichthyosiform erythroderma, epidermolytic hyperkeratosis, or lamellar ichthyosis may be born with a collodion membrane, which peels off after a week. Careful management is needed due to impaired skin barrier function.

Acquired ichthyosis can result from lymphoma, HIV, leprosy, essential fatty acid deficiency, or lipid-lowering drugs.

Darier's disease is rare and autosomal dominant or sporadic, featuring brownish, scaly papules on the upper body and face, along with palmar pits and nail dystrophy.

Tuberous sclerosis is a rare autosomal dominant disorder with multiple anomalies, including pink-red papules around the nose and cheeks.

Von Recklinghausen disease (neurofibromatosis) is a common autosomal dominant disorder characterized by soft, pink tumors and "cafe au lait" spots.

Ichthyosis Vulgaris, commonly known as "fish scale disease," is a skin condition where dead skin cells accumulate, creating fish-scale-like patches.

The disease is caused by defective genes inherited from parents. Symptoms may appear shortly after birth or during childhood.

Symptoms of Ichthyosis include dry skin, fish-scale-like patches, and thickened skin, typically on the elbows and knees but sometimes extending to the arms and hands.
